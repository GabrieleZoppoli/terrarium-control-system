*Darlingtonia californica* — the California Pitcher Plant — has a notorious cultivation requirement that most growers struggle with: it needs **cool roots**, even when air temperatures rise. In its native habitat (cold mountain seeps in Oregon and Northern California), constantly flowing cold water from underground springs keeps the rhizome below ~18 °C while air temperatures may exceed 30 °C.

The standard hobbyist solution is to flush the pot with cold water daily or to use ice cubes in summer. Both are fragile: a missed day can be fatal.

## The Zeer pot solution

A Zeer pot (or pot-in-pot cooler) uses two unglazed clay pots nested with wet sand between them. Water evaporates from the outer pot's surface, drawing heat from the inner pot — the same physics as wet-bulb cooling, with no electricity required.

This project adapts the Zeer principle to *Darlingtonia*:
- **Inner pot**: Holds the plant in its growing substrate (long-fiber sphagnum + perlite)
- **Sand annulus**: Between the inner and outer pots, kept saturated with water
- **Outer pot**: Unglazed terracotta — the evaporating surface
- **Self-watering reservoir**: Float-valve fed from a 5-liter tank, automatically tops up the sand to maintain saturation

The expected cooling effect is the difference between dry-bulb and wet-bulb air temperatures — typically 4–8 °C in temperate summers — which is enough to bring root-zone temperatures into the safe range for *Darlingtonia* without active refrigeration.
