*Dracula pholeodytes* is not the loudest plant in my highland cabinet. It's smaller than the *vlad-tepes* nearby, quieter than *simia* and its monkey face. Open the glass door when it's in flower, though, and it makes you lean in. The flower presents as a hood: the three sepals fold forward and meet in front, sealing off the column and labellum behind them, so what you see first is a little dark chamber with a pale thing tucked inside. The pale thing is the lip. The chamber is the flower.

{{< figure src="/img/collection/dracula/dracula-pholeodytes.jpg" caption="The hooded presentation — three sepals curved inward, the labellum sitting inside the pouch like a small pale mushroom. Finger at lower right for scale." >}}

## The name earns itself

*Pholeodytes*, from Greek *pholeos* (cave, den, lair) and *dytes* (diver, one who dives): the cave-diver. Most *Dracula* species go for theatre — the long-tailed *simia* and *vampira* with their "faces" centred in the flower, or the spread-petal big ones. *Pholeodytes* goes the other way. It hides: the pouch is the presentation, the sexual parts are tucked inside. IOSPE glosses the name as referring to *"the central apparatus within the deeply cupped black interior of the flower"*.[^iospe]

It's also a miniature by *Dracula* standards — around 3.75 cm across the flower, the plant a compact tuft of narrow leaves on short ramicauls.[^iospe]

## Where it's from

**North-eastern Colombia**, Boyacá department,[^iospe] on the eastern Cordillera of the Andes — **not** the Chocó despite the genus' association with western-slope cloud forests. Kew's Plants of the World Online gives the native range simply as "NE. Colombia".[^powo] Elevation around 2,500 m, which is why IOSPE classifies it as **cold-growing** rather than cool.[^iospe] The eastern Andes there run in subpáramo / elfin forest territory — perpetually damp, foggy, cold by the standards of the tropics.

First described by **Carl Luer & Rodrigo Escobar, *Selbyana* 7: 68 (1982)**.[^powo] A relatively recent addition to the genus, published four years after Luer's 1978 splitting of *Dracula* out of *Masdevallia*.

## How it got here

From [Ecuagenera](https://ecuagenera.com) in early 2023, as a small division on sphagnum about the size of a 2 € coin. Took most of 2023 to settle in. Mounted on a cork slab now with a light sphagnum wrap at the base, in the shaded lower third of the [highland cabinet](/highland/) where condensation runs down the back wall most evenings.

## Cultivation notes

- **Temperature.** Cold-grower. The cabinet tracks Colombian weather on a 15-hour offset; *pholeodytes* sees roughly 13.5–15 °C at night, 19–21 °C peak day. At 2,500 m on the eastern Cordillera, wild plants experience nights routinely below 10 °C — but in cultivation most growers aim for 12–15 °C minima. Warm nights kill it.
- **Humidity.** 85–98 % RH, effectively always. IOSPE lists it as "full shade" — so humidity rather than light is the primary lever.[^iospe]
- **Air.** Constant gentle movement. Still, humid air is what produces the fungal rots these plants can't tolerate.
- **Light.** Shade. It grows in the dimmer part of the cabinet, behind taller residents.
- **Water.** Rainwater or RO. The sphagnum never dries; the mount is re-wetted a few times a week by the misting cycle.

## Curios

- The genus *Dracula* was split from *Masdevallia* by Carl Luer in 1978. The split was botanical but also atmospheric — Luer thought these orchids deserved their own genus for the sheer strangeness of their flowers. (The name is a straight-up horror reference.)
- Several *Dracula* species are known to **mimic fungi** in flower shape and scent, recruiting fungus gnats as pollinators instead of bees. The definitive experimental work is Endara, Grimaldi & Roy (2010) on *D. lafleurii* — small drosophilid flies visited its labellum, which smelled and looked enough like a real mushroom to fool them.[^endara] The pale fungus-gill-like labellum inside the *pholeodytes* pouch is consistent with that syndrome, but I'm not aware of direct pollination work on this species specifically.
- *Pholeodytes* comes from the eastern side of the Colombian Andes — a region that yields fewer orchids to cultivation than the famously wet Pacific Chocó to the west. Seeing it on a cork mount 8,000 km from home still feels like a small transport miracle.
