*Dracula pholeodytes* is a strange little thing. The head of the flower is a thumbnail-sized maroon-speckled dome with a pale skull-shaped monkey face tucked inside. Each of the three sepals ends in a long thin tail; the three tails fan out in three directions across 10–15 cm. In the hand it looks like a flat purple spider with a face painted on it. I like it a lot.

{{< figure src="/img/collection/dracula/dracula-pholeodytes.jpg" caption="Cup between thumb and forefinger. Two tails going up, one going down, one monkey staring out of the middle." >}}

## What the tails actually are

Those long thin things are **caudae** (singular *cauda*, "tail") — elongated tips of the **sepals**. They are not petals. Petals in *Dracula* are the two tiny nubs flanking the column *inside* the cup, and in *pholeodytes* they are basically invisible. The sepal caudae do all the visual work. IOSPE puts the whole flower at 1.5 × 6 in (3.75 × 15 cm), and yes — most of that 15 cm is tail.[^iospe]

## Why the name is actually good

*Pholeodytes* = *pholeos* (cave, den) + *dytes* (diver). "Cave-diver." Carl Luer gave it the name because the monkey face — really the column + labellum — sits deep inside the cup rather than perched at the front like on *simia*, *gigas* or *vampira*. So it's a diving monkey. Orchid taxonomy at its peak.

## Home territory

North-eastern Colombia, Boyacá department,[^iospe] on the **eastern** slope of the Andes. Not the Chocó, even though cloud-forest *Dracula* have a Chocó reputation. POWO gives the range simply as "NE. Colombia".[^powo] Around 2,500 m — which is why IOSPE tags it cold-growing (not "cool". Cold).[^iospe]

Described by Luer and Rodrigo Escobar in *Selbyana* 7: 68 (1982),[^powo] four years after Luer split *Dracula* out of *Masdevallia* on the grounds of "these flowers are weird enough to deserve their own genus". Unusually honest as genus-level-split rationales go.

## How mine got here

From Ecuagenera, early 2023. It arrived as a 2 € coin of sphagnum and a bad attitude. Took a year to settle in. Now lives on a cork slab in the bottom third of the [highland cabinet](/highland/), in the wettest, darkest corner.

## Growing it (if you're masochistic)

- **Cold nights.** 12–15 °C minimum. If your indoor summer nights run above 18 °C, don't buy one. There is no workaround that isn't a refrigerated cabinet.
- **Humidity** at 85–98 % RH, basically always. IOSPE says full shade; believe them.[^iospe]
- **Air moving constantly.** Still wet air is what produces the crown rots everybody on the CP and orchid forums complains about.
- **Rainwater or RO** only. Sphagnum never dries.

I've killed easier plants. Worth it.

## A few notes

- Several *Dracula* species look and smell like **fungi**, and get pollinated by fungus-infesting flies rather than bees. Endara, Grimaldi & Roy (2010) showed this experimentally for *D. lafleurii*.[^endara] The pale labellum in *pholeodytes* looks exactly like a tiny gilled mushroom, so the syndrome probably applies here too. Nobody's done the fieldwork.
- "Monkey face" is a whole-genus thing, not a *simia*-specific thing. What's particular about *pholeodytes* is that the face sits *inside* the pouch rather than out front — exactly what "cave-diver" is trying to describe.
- The Colombian eastern Andes are nobody's orchid hotspot compared to the Chocó on the Pacific slope. A cultivated *pholeodytes* is an actual mountain plant from a region that exports almost nothing to the hobby.
