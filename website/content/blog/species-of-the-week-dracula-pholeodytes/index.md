*Dracula pholeodytes* is not the loudest plant in my highland cabinet. It's smaller than the *vlad-tepes* nearby, quieter than *simia* and its monkey face, more hesitant than the plates-for-petals *gigas* I don't own. But open the glass door when it's in flower and it makes you lean in. The flower presents as a hood: the three sepals fold forward and meet in front, sealing off the column and labellum behind them, so what you see first is a little dark chamber with a pale thing tucked inside. The pale thing is the lip. The chamber is the flower.

## The name earns itself

*Pholeodytes*, from Greek *pholeos* (cave, den, lair) and *dytes* (diver, one who dives): the cave-diver. Carl Luer named it for the pouch-presentation, and when you see it in person you understand why. Most *Dracula* species go for theatre — the long-tailed *simia* and *vampira* with their "faces" centred in the flower, or the spread-petal big ones like *gigas*. *Pholeodytes* goes the other way. It hides.

It's also a miniature — flowers around 3-4 cm across, the plant a compact tuft of narrow leaves, a good fit for a mounted spot in a compact highland cabinet where shelf space is tight.

## Where it's from

Colombia, per [Kew's Plants of the World Online](https://powo.science.kew.org/results?q=Dracula%20pholeodytes) — endemic to the Colombian Chocó, the biogeographic region that runs down the western slope of the Andes from Panama into Ecuador. Depending on which part of the Chocó you're in you can find some of the wettest places on Earth — parts of the region record over 10 metres of rain a year. That's the climate the plant evolved in: constantly saturated air, cool, dim, with organic matter thick on every surface. A lot of Draculas share that envelope, and nearly all of them die in a warm grow tent.

The plant likely occurs between about 1,500 and 2,000 m. Older sources occasionally list Ecuador too; POWO's current synthesis gives Colombia only and that's what I'll go with.

## How it got here

From [Ecuagenera's Europe branch](https://ecuagenera.com) in early 2023 — one of the more reliable routes into the smaller Pleurothallid orchids, species that don't travel well through the usual orchid-import channels. It arrived as a small division on sphagnum, about the size of a 2 € coin, and took most of 2023 to settle in. Mounted on a cork slab with a light sphagnum wrap at the base now, in the shaded lower third of the [highland cabinet](/highland/), where condensation runs down the back wall most evenings.

## Cultivation notes (if you're trying it)

- **Temperature.** The cabinet tracks Colombian weather on a 15-hour offset; *pholeodytes* sees roughly 13.5–15 °C at night, 19–21 °C peak in the day. It will not tolerate warm nights. A Genoese living room in August would kill it in a week.
- **Humidity.** 85–98 % RH, effectively always. Hesitation at lower humidity shows first in wrinkled leaf tips.
- **Air.** Constant gentle movement. Still, humid, warm air is what produces the fungal rots these plants can't tolerate.
- **Light.** Shade. It grows in the dimmer part of the cabinet, behind taller residents.
- **Water.** Rainwater or RO only. The sphagnum never dries; the mount is re-wetted a few times a week by the misting cycle.

## Curios

- The genus *Dracula* was split from *Masdevallia* by Carl Luer in 1978. The split was botanical but also atmospheric — Luer thought these orchids deserved their own genus for the sheer strangeness of their flowers. (The name is a straight-up horror reference.)
- Several Draculas appear to mimic fungi in flower shape and scent, recruiting fungus gnats (Drosophilidae, some mycophagous Diptera) as pollinators instead of bees. The pale labellum centred in the pouch of *pholeodytes* is consistent with that syndrome — it looks like a small gill-plate mushroom in a wet hollow.
- The Chocó is so wet in places that botanists measure rainfall there in double-digit metres per year. Annual averages over 10 m are documented at some weather stations on the Colombian Pacific slope. Most of that water falls at night.
