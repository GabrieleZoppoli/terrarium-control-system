*Dracula pholeodytes* runs smaller than the *Raven 'Jet'* next to it, but small here doesn't mean subdued. The three sepals extend into thin, dark **caudae** — the genus-defining tails — trailing the flower out to roughly 15 cm of vertical drama. Open the glass and what you see first is the sepaline cup: a heart-shaped, dark-maroon-speckled hood opening forward. Deep inside the cup sits a pale, skull-like labellum — the "monkey face" that has made *Dracula* a cult genus. *Pholeodytes* is, for my money, one of the coolest monkeys in it.

## A quick anatomy note

The long, thin "whiskers" or "spurs" people usually point at on a *Dracula* flower are **caudae** — elongated tips of the **sepals** (the outer three tepals). They are not petals. The petals in *Dracula* are the two much smaller structures flanking the column *inside* the cup. In *pholeodytes* the three caudae do most of the visual work: per IOSPE the flower measures about 1.5 × 6 inches (≈ 3.75 × 15 cm), and almost all of that 15 cm is tails hanging below the cup.[^iospe]

## The name earns itself

*Pholeodytes*, from Greek *pholeos* (cave, den, lair) and *dytes* (diver, one who dives): the cave-diver. IOSPE glosses the name as referring to *"the central apparatus within the deeply cupped black interior of the flower"* — the column and labellum tucked inside the speckled cup.[^iospe] So the "cave-diver" is the pale face you see when you look down into the hood: the flower is the cave, the column-face is the diver.

## Where it's from

**North-eastern Colombia**, Boyacá department,[^iospe] on the eastern Cordillera of the Andes — *not* the Chocó, despite the genus' associations with western-slope cloud forests. POWO gives the native range simply as "NE. Colombia".[^powo] Elevation around 2,500 m, which is why IOSPE classifies it as **cold-growing** rather than cool.[^iospe] The eastern Andes there run in subpáramo / elfin forest territory — perpetually damp, foggy, cold by tropical standards.

First described by **Carl Luer & Rodrigo Escobar, *Selbyana* 7: 68 (1982)**.[^powo] A relatively recent addition to the genus, four years after Luer's 1978 splitting of *Dracula* out of *Masdevallia*.

## How it got here

From [Ecuagenera](https://ecuagenera.com) in early 2023 — a small division on sphagnum the size of a 2 € coin. Took most of 2023 to settle in. Mounted on a cork slab now with a light sphagnum wrap at the base, in the shaded lower third of the [highland cabinet](/highland/) where condensation runs down the back wall most evenings.

## Cultivation notes

- **Temperature.** Cold-grower. The cabinet tracks Colombian weather on a 15-hour offset; *pholeodytes* sees roughly 13.5–15 °C at night, 19–21 °C peak day. At 2,500 m on the eastern Cordillera, wild plants see nights routinely below 10 °C — in cultivation most aim for 12–15 °C minima. Warm nights kill it.
- **Humidity.** 85–98 % RH, effectively always. IOSPE lists it as "full shade", so humidity more than light is the primary lever.[^iospe]
- **Air.** Constant gentle movement. Still, humid air is what produces the fungal rots these plants can't tolerate.
- **Light.** Shade. It grows in the dimmer part of the cabinet, behind taller residents.
- **Water.** Rainwater or RO. The sphagnum never dries; the mount is re-wetted a few times a week by the misting cycle.

## Curios

- The genus *Dracula* was split from *Masdevallia* by Carl Luer in 1978. The split was botanical but also atmospheric — Luer thought these orchids deserved their own genus for the sheer strangeness of their flowers. (The name is a straight horror reference.)
- The **monkey-face** impression is shared across many *Dracula* — *simia*, *gigas*, *vampira*, *lotax*, *pholeodytes*, and so on. Each species positions its column + labellum + sepal-markings differently, and the "face" that emerges is its fingerprint. *Pholeodytes'* face is deep-set inside the pouch rather than perched at the front of the flower, which is what the "cave-diver" name captures.
- Several *Dracula* species are known to **mimic fungi** in flower shape and scent, recruiting fungus gnats as pollinators instead of bees. The definitive experimental work is Endara, Grimaldi & Roy (2010) on *D. lafleurii* — drosophilid flies were shown to visit its labellum, which looked and smelled enough like a real mushroom to fool them.[^endara] The pale fungus-gill-like labellum inside the *pholeodytes* pouch is consistent with that syndrome; I'm not aware of direct pollination work on this species specifically.
- *Pholeodytes* comes from the **eastern** side of the Colombian Andes — a region that yields far fewer orchids to cultivation than the famously wet Pacific Chocó to the west. Seeing it on a cork mount 8,000 km from home still feels like a small transport miracle.
